4A.
Fundamental Stress-Strain Relationships (cont.)
4A.5 Effective Stress Principle    

Effective stress is the difference between the total stress and pore-water pressure (u). Total stress is based on the total weight per unit area of the soil, including water in the soil structure. Effective stress results from the buoyant force of water on soil particles (Archimedes' principle). Figure 4A.7 shows an infinitesimally small element at a depth Z below the ground surface and at a depth Zw below a static ground water table. It also shows a total stress and pore-water pressure perpendicular to any plane at element A in the soil profile. The total vertical stress, σv, acting on this element consists of the total weight above the element:
σv = (Z - Zw) γ + Zwγsat
(4A.3)

where:
γ = soil unit weight (pcf)
γ sat = saturated soil unit weight (pcf).

In addition to the total stress there is a water pressure, u, acting on this element:

u = Zwγw
 (4A.4)

where:
γw = unit weight of water (pcf).

Figure 4A.7.—Stresses in the subsoil (reprinted with permission of John Wiley & Sons from Soil Mechanics by T.W. Lambe and R.V. Whitman. Copyright © 1964 John Wiley & Sons.)

   

The vertical effective stress then equals:
σ = σv - u       (4A.5)

The following examples and figure 4A.8 illustrate the calculation of effective stress. Additional discussion can be found in sections 4E.1.1 and 4D.2.4.

Figure 4A.8.—Calculation of effective stress (reprinted with permission of John Wiley & Sons from Fundamentals of geotechnical analysis by I.S. Dunn, L.R. Anderson, and F.W. Kiefer. Copyright © 1980 John Wiley & Sons.)
 

Example 1



u1 = 10ft * 62.4 lb/ft3 = 624 lb/ft2
u1 = 9ft * 62.4 lb/ft3 = 561.6 lb/ft2

Resultant buoyant force,
B = u1(area of block) - u2(area of block)
= 624 lb/ft2(1ft2) - 561.6 lb/ft2(1ft2)
= 62.4 lb

Buoyant weight = 100 lb - 62.4 lb = 37.6 lb

In terms of stresses:

Total stress = 9 ft (62.4 lb/ft3) + 1 ft(100 lb/ft3) = 661.6 lb/ft2

Water pressure = 10 ft * 62.4 lb/ft3 = 624 lb/ft3

Effective stress = Total stress - water pressure
= 661.6 lb/ft2 - 624 lb/ft2 = 37.6 lb/ft2

(After Dunn et al., 1980)
 

Example 2

Total stress:
σ = γ t * Z = 120 pcf (20 ft) = 2400 psf

Pore pressure:
u = γ w * Zw = 62.4 pcf (20 ft) = 1248 psf

Effective stress:
σ' = σ - u = 2400 psf - 1248 psf = 1152 psf
4A.6 Shear Strength Parameters and Failure Criteria
   
4A.6.1 Angle of Internal Friction, φ   

The shear resistance resulting from friction can be illustrated by a free-body diagram of a stationary block on an inclined plane.


Resisting shear force: S = W sin α

Normal force: N = W cos α

The weight of the block is met by a resisting force of equal magnitude and opposite direction at the surface of the inclined plane. This resisting force can be separated into components parallel to the inclined plane (S) and normal to the inclined plane (N) as shown in the force triangle. If the angle α is increased, at some point the block will be just ready to slide. The value of α at the point of incipient slip is known as the friction angle, φ, and tan φ = S/N is the coefficient of friction. An expanded discussion of friction and friction angle can be found in sections 4D.2.1 and 4D.2.2. In terms of stress,

tan φ = τ/σ    

(4A.6)

τ = σ tan φ      

(4A.7)

where:

τ = resisting shear stress
σ = normal stress

4A.6.2 Cohesion         

In equation 4A.6, the resisting shear stress is proportional to the normal stress. There are some situations in which part of the total shear resistance between particles is independent of the normal force; that is, even if the normal force is decreased to zero, there is still a measurable shear resistance (Lambe and Whitman, 1969). This shear resistance, when the normal force between two bodies is zero, is known as true cohesion. Cohesion, c, is in units of stress, such as psi or psf.

Coulomb (ca. 1773) presented the first hypothesis on the shear strength, S, of soil as a combination of cohesion and frictional resistance:

S = c + σ tan φ

 (4A.8)

or, in terms of effective stress:

S = c + (σ - u)tan φ = c + σtan φ      

(4A.9)

where c and φ are effective strength parameters.

Mohr (ca. 1882) presented a theory of failure that states that while failure is essentially by shear, the critical shear stress, τ, is a function of the normal stress acting on a potential surface of failure (Bowles, 1984):

τ = f (σ).

 (4A.10)

One function of the normal stress is the Coulomb equation:

τ = c + σ tan φ.

(4A.11)

Today this function is commonly known as the Mohr-Coulomb failure criterion and is the most widely used method to define failure in soils. When plotted on the σ and τ coordinates, the critical combinations of shear stress (τ) and normal stress (σt) will form a line known as the Mohr-Coulomb failure envelope.

Figure 4A.9 illustrates the Mohr-Coulomb failure envelope. In this case, two blocks with a normal stress between them are subjected to an increasing shear stress until movement occurs along the contact plane. The stress at which movement occurs is recorded. If this test is repeated several times using different normal stresses, the resulting shear stress can be plotted as shown. Cohesion and friction angle can be determined from the plot. Additional discussion on the Mohr-Coulomb failure criterion can be found in section 4D.2.3.

4A.7 Conclusion

Stability analysis simply compares the material's shear strength available to resist failure to shear forces (stresses) that are present to cause failure. The remainder of section 4 is designed to assist in selecting the parameters used in the analysis techniques presented in sections 5 and 6.
