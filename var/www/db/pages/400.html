Plant Species: Montia perfoliata, miner's lettuce

Kingdom: Plantae
Phylum: Magnoliophyta -- flowering plants
Class: Magnoliopsida -- dicotyledons
Family: Portulacaceae
Genus: Montia
Species: perfoliata
Variety:
Common Name: miner’s lettuce
Species Code: MOPE, CLPE
Origin: Native to places which are vernally moist and often partially shaded from British Columbia to Baja California and east to Colorado and Montana.
Rare: no

Form: forb, annual from a taproot, stems 5-35 cm tall, arising from the base, simple above, ascending to erect.
Duration: annual
Longevity: annual
Habitat Type: prairie, forest, shrub thickets
Wetland Indicator Status: not listed

Leaves: basal leaves variable, few to numerous in a suberect to erect rosette, petiolate, subequal, broadly rhombic to deltate to spatulate, blade often weakly red pigmented; cauline leaves opposite, usually connate, forming a disc around the stem.
Mature height: 2-14 inches
Flowers: borne in a raceme; sepals 2, 1.5- 4 mm long, accrescent to 6 mm; petals 5, white or pink, 2-6 mm long.
Flower color: white, pink
Bloom: May, June, July
Bloom starts on: May
Bloom ends on: July
Fruit: capsule; seeds rotund to ovoid, black, shiny, 1-2.7 mm long with a white elaiosome.
Vegetation type:
Characteristics:
Claytonia perfoliata is synonymous. Montia and Claytonia are usually separated on the basis of the latter having corms or thick, fleshy roots. However, some taxonomists do not consider this a basis for separation and include Montia in Claytonia.
Reproduces sexually by seed.
Plant is an annual.
1,099,903 seeds/lb (USDA NRCS Pullman PMC 2005).
x=6.
2n = 12, 24, 36, 48, 60 (Flora of North America Editorial Committee 1993+).
Bees and bumblebees are common visitors, other Hymenoptera and Diptera also visit the flowers (Miller 1976).
Flowers are perfect.
Diploids, tetraploids, hexaploids, octoploids, and decaploids are known.
Aneuploidy was not found (Miller 1976, Miller 1978).
Both polyploidy and aneuploidy are known in Claytonia perfoliata var. perfoliata (University of British Columbia 2003).
Leaves and stems were widely used for greens, either raw or cooked, by Native Americans, miners and early settlers.
Fruit is a capsule.
Mostly autogamous but some outcrossing may occur (Miller 1976). Xenogamy is more common among some forms and ploidy levels, while others are mostly autogamous (Miller 1978).
Seeds are expelled explosively and also possess an elaiosome attractive to ants.

Notes:
Montia perfoliata is a polyploid complex with highly variable morphology. The form sometimes referred to as Montia (Claytonia) rubra is apparently part of this complex. It is distinguished by leaves with strong red pigmentation and cauline leaves which are strongly connate on one side only. It also seems to have a stronger affinity for growing in full sun than typical M. perfoliata.
