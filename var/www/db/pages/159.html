Characteristics:
Leymus cinereus is the accepted name for the taxon. It is treated as Elymus in many older floras, but more recent genetic evidence shows it does not contain the proper genome to be included there.
E. condensatus in Piper & Beattie 1914.
Reproduces sexually by seed. Occasionally has short rhizomes.
95,000-166,000 seeds/lb (Hassell et al 1996).
130,000 seeds/lb (USDA NRCS PLANTS Database 2006).
Has some tolerance of saline and/or sodic soils.
Most often found in low areas with deep soils which receive some water runoff from adjacent areas.
2n=28, 56.
Wind pollinated.
Flowers are perfect.
Fruit is a caryopsis.
All species of Leymus are self-incompatible, outcrossing polyploids (Barkworth et al 2002).
Diploids and tetraploids exist.
Ergot (Claviceps purpurea) may infest the seedheads.
Native peoples had medicinal uses (Parish et al 1996).
Poor forage but elk will feed on it. Rodents eat the seeds (Craighead et al 1963).
Good forage for livestock but the growing point is elevated, making it very susceptible to overgrazing.
Birds use the cover for nesting and winter habitat and deer use it for bedding.
Many grasses are hosts for the larva of the common wood nymph (Cercyonis pegala) and the common ringlet (Coenonympha californica) butterflies (Pocewicz 2005).
Fair to good forage for livestock and wildlife in spring, becoming rough and unpalatable in summer, but good standing forage in winter (Roche 1991, Stubbendieck 1997).

Other Propagation Information:
Seeds are not dormant in autumn but are slow to emerge. They emerge mostly in the spring (Meyer et al 1995).
Exposure of seeds to smoke increases growth of plants (Blank & Young 1998).
Germination, seed weight, percent seed fill, and soil characteristics are not correlated (Young & Evans 1981).
For an alpine seed source, cold moist stratification and gibberellic acid increased germination (McDonough 1969).
