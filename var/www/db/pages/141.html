Origin: Native to moist rock crevices in open woods and in adjoining grasslands from southern British Columbia south to California and east to the western parts of Utah and Montana.
Rare: no

Form: fern, perennial from a much branched rhizome, decumbent. Gametophytes glabrous.
Duration: perennial
Longevity:
Habitat Type: prairie, dry forest
Wetland Indicator Status: not listed

Leaves: evergreen; petioles 2-12 cm long, brown; leaves bipinnate; pinnae crowded, oblong-lanceolate, glabrate above, brown scaly beneath; pinnules small, longer than wide, brown wooly on the underside.
Mature height:
Sporulates: late spring to early summer (Soltis et al 1989).
Fruit: reproduces by spores; sori borne at vein ends near the margin of the pinnules, the margins reflexed over the sori to form a false indusium; spores tetrahedral.
Vegetation type:

Characteristics:
The original family Polypodiaceae has been divided into a number families and Cheilanthes placed in the family Pteridaceae.
Reproduces sexually by spores and vegetatively by rhizomes.
Spores are haploid. They germinate to form a gametophyte generation called a prothallus. The prothallus contains the sexual organs and sexual reproduction occurs there. Successful fertilization requires water between the prothallus and the soil. The fertilized egg develops into a new plant, the sporophyte (diploid) generation.
x=30 (Flora of North America Editorial Committee 1993+).
Highly outcrossed (Soltis & Soltis 1990).
Spores are windborne.
Perennating organ is a rhizome.
