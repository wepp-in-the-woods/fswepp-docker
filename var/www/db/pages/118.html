Plant Species: Arnica sororia, foothills arnica

Kingdom: Plantae
Phylum: Magnoliophyta -- flowering plants
Class: Magnoliopsida -- dicots
Family: Asteraceae (Compositae)
Genus: Arnica
Species: sororia
Variety:
Common Name: foothills arnica, twin arnica
Species Code: ARSO4
Origin: Native to open meadows and grasslands of western North America from British Columbia and Alberta south to Wyoming and the Great Basin area of California, Nevada and Utah.
Rare: no

Form: forb, perennial, erect, 20-60 cm tall, sparsely to densely hairy throughout, rhizomatous.
Duration: perennial
Longevity: medium
Habitat Type: prairie
Wetland Indicator Status: not listed

Leaves: basal and lower leaves petiolate, alternate, oblanceolate to elliptical, entire, 5-12 cm long; cauline leaves opposite, sessile, lanceolate, reduced upward.
Mature height: 12-20 inches
Flowers: yellow, borne singly or in a cyme, ray flowers pistillate, disc flowers complete.
Flower color: yellow
Bloom: May, June
Bloom starts on: May
Bloom ends on: June
Fruit: achene, 4-5 mm long, fusiform, black, hairy; pappus usually white, barbellate.
Vegetation type:
Characteristics:
A. sororia is similar to A. fulgens and is sometimes regarded as a variety thereof, Arnica fulgens var. sororia. It differs from A. fulgens in having somewhat narrower leaves and involucral bracts, usually lacking dense tufts of hair in the leaf axils, or, if hairs are present, they are white rather than brown.
Reproduces both sexually by seed and vegetatively by rhizomes.

Ray flowers are pistillate, disc flowers are complete.
Fruit is an achene.
Rocky Mountain elk graze the plants in spring (Kufeld 1973).
Seeds are probably dispersed short distances by wind.

Notes: Arnica sororia could be used more in landscaping. The yellow flowers are vibrant. Not easy from seed, but not really difficult either. It blooms in June, then goes dormant for the hot part of the summer. Weakly rhizomatous. Sow outdoors in pots in the fall. Transplant in early spring (Skinner et al 2005).
