Common Name: fireweed
Origin: A circumboreal species of Europe, Asia, and North America. In North America it is found on moist soils of prairies, open woods, and riparian areas from Alaska south to California and east to the Atlantic Coast. In forested areas it is especially common in recently burned areas and along roads. The Palouse is at the lower end of its ecological amplitude and it occurs here only on sites with soil moisture late in the season.

Diploids are found in northern Canada and alpine regions while tetraploids occur in temperate regions. Hexaploids are also found in the warm regions of Asia (Mosquin 1967).
In the genus, polyploids are only know to occur in E. angustifolium and E. latifolium (Mosquin & Small 1971).
Primarily pollinated by bees, which are highly attracted to the flowers. Honeybees make an excellent honey from the nectar.

Flowers are self compatible (Mosquin 1966) but protandry reduces self pollination (Broderick 1990).
Self compatible but cross pollination does occur (Mitich 1999).
Seeds are windborne and may be transported long distances (Solbreck & Andersson 1987).
Young shoots are edible.
Deer, moose, caribou, cattle, muskrats and rabbits eat the plants but it will not withstand heavy grazing and trampling (Mitich 1999). It is also forage for grizzly bears (Craighead et al 1963), and for Rocky Mountain elk in summer (Kufeld 1973). Poor to good forage for livestock, minor forage for deer and elk when young (Stubbendieck 1997). A number of phytophagous insects feed on the plants (Broderick 1990).
