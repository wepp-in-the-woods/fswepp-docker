Common Name: fern-leaf biscuitroot
Origin: Native to dry, open areas from sagebrush steppe to mesic grasslands of western North America ranging from southern interior British Columbia south to California and east to New Mexico and Montana. The common Palouse phase is var. multifidum.
Rare: L. dissectum var. multifidum is common. L. dissectum var. dissectum was a monitored species in Idaho until 2002 when the Idaho Conservation Data Center determined that it was “more common than previously thought.”

Leptotaenia multifida in Piper & Beattie 1914.
Reproduces sexually by seed.
L. dissectum var. multifidum is the common Palouse phase. L. dissectum var. dissectum is mostly found on the east slope of the Cascades but there are disjunct populations in northern Idaho. The schizocarps of L. dissectum var. multifidum are borne on distinct pedicels, while the schizocarps of var. dissectum are sessile or on very short pedicels.
Flowers are usually yellow but occasionally purple.
45,031 seeds/lb (USDA NRCS Pullman PMC 2005).
Perennating organ is a woody taproot, or sometimes a caudex above the taproot.
2n=22 for both var. multifidum and var. dissectum (Baldwin et al 2004).
Plants are andromonoecious, having both perfect and staminate flowers.
Perfect flowers are protogynous (Schlessman & Barrie 2004).
Deer, elk, and rodents eat the foliage in the spring (Craighead et al 1963).
The roots of Lomatium dissectum were an important food of the Nez Perce and other Columbia Plateau people (Mastrogiuseppe 2000).
Larva of the moth Depressaria leptotaeniae feed on flower buds, umbel sheaths, umbels, immature seeds, and leaves (Thompson 1983).
Dispersed seeds are consumed by ground beetles and small mammals, which prefer L. dissectum seeds over those of L. triternatum (Thompson 1985).
Pocket gophers feed on the roots, frequently killing the plant. White-tailed deer and rabbits graze on the plants (Thompson 1998).
A gall fly, Contarinia sp., oviposits in the floral buds, causing galls containing the larva. A leaf miner, Phytomyza sp. oviposits in the leaves. A rust fungus, Puccinia sp infects leaves and stems. Aphids and butterfly larva are minor feeders on L. dissectum (Thompson 1998).
Plants do not flower every year (Thompson 1998).
Fruit is a schizocarp, separating into two mericarps (seeds) at maturity.
Seeds are probably windborne for short distances.
Larva of the moth Depressaria leptotaeniae (Lepidoptera) feeds on all plant parts, including immature seeds (Thompson 1983).
Larva of the moth Greya subalba consume immature seeds (Thompson 1986, 1987, Althoff & Thompson 1999).
A braconid wasp, Agathis thompsoni, is a parasitoid of Greya subalba (Thompson 1986, Althoff & Thompson 1999).
Lomatium species are a host for the larva of the indra swallowtail butterfly, Papilo indra, and for the larva of the anise swallowtail, Papilio zelicaon (Pocewicz 2005).
