The conifers are gymnosperms and do not have flowers in the traditional sense. Instead, the male and female reproductive parts are borne in separate strobili (cones) on the same tree. The male cones drop from the tree soon after the pollen is released.
Larix occidentalis is unique among local conifers in that it sheds its needles every fall.
Seeds are windborne, at least for short distances.
A valuable timber species and for firewood. Native peoples used various plant parts for medicinal purposes and some used the dried sap for food.
Twigs are occasionally browsed by deer and grouse eat the needles (Petrides 1998). Provides cover and shelter for wildlife.
Very shade intolerant.
The thick bark and the tendency to self-prune the lower limbs make the species fire resistant. May live as long as 900 years (Petrides 1998).
