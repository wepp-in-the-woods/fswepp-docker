Heracleum maximum W. Bartram is the currently accepted name.
Heracleum sphondylium L. ssp. montanum (Gaudin) Briquet in Schinz & Keller and H. sphondylium L. var. lanatum (Michx.) Dorn are synonymous.

47,569 seeds/lb (USDA NRCS PLANTS Database 2007).
44,000-76,000 seeds/lb (Hassell et al 1996).
36,621 seeds/lb (USDA NRCS Pullman PMC 2005).
H. lanatum is andromonoecious, producing both perfect and staminate flowers in the same umbel.
The proportions of staminate and perfect flowers in an inflorescence varies according to position in the umbel and whether the plant is growing in shade or sun (Cruden 1976).
Reproduces sexually by seed.
Grows from a taproot or a tight cluster of fibrous roots.
2n=22.
Diploids only are known.
After peeling, young stems can be eaten, but the outer surface of the stems may cause skin irritation. Native Americans also had medicinal uses for the plant.
Fruit is a schizocarp.
Flowers within an umbel are either staminate or protandrous and mature at the same time (Schlessman & Barrie 2004). This results in the plant being functionally dioecious at any given time (Hendrix 1984).
Seeds are windborne for short distances.
Livestock and large ungulates graze the plants.
The parsnip webworm, Depressaria pastinacella, introduced from Europe, feeds on the developing umbels.
H. lanatum reacts to predation by Depressaria pastinacella by increasing the number of perfect flowers in late developing umbels, but total seed production and seed biomass are lowered (Hendrix 1984).
H. lanatum is a host for larva of the anise swallowtail butterfly, Papilio zelecaon (Pocewicz 2005).
Pollinated by bees and flies (Cruden 1976).

Other Propagation Information: Seed in fall. Slow to establish. Reseeds well (Kingery et al 2003).
Grow from seeds planted soon after harvest (Kruckeberg 1996).
Stored seed at 3oC maintains germination for 3 years. Stratification is not required but seed should be leached for 4 hours in running tap water. Needs fluctuating temperatures of 22oC for 8 hours and 17oC for 16 hours for germination (Rose et al 1998).
Seed of a California ecotype germinates without pretreatment (Mirov 1939).
96% germination when seeds are leached for 4 hours in running tap water, then given 16 weeks of cold moist stratification and germinated at alternating temperatures of 22/17oC. Untreated seeds had virtually no germination (McDonough 1969).
Seed stores up to 3 years in unheated warehouse, although germination was always low (7-8%) with the techniques used (Stevens et al 1981).
Untreated seed did not germinate at any temperature regime (Maguire & Overland 1959). They did not try stratification.

Notes: Usually riparian, wetland, or hawthorn thickets; sometimes occurs on moist sites in prairie where there is apparently subsurface moisture.
Heracleum lanatum grows very large, sometimes over 6 feet tall, and thus may not be a good plant for small gardens. Probably better for semi-wild areas. Will grow in sun or shade where soils are moist. Flowers in mid to late June. Other names include Heracleum maximum, cowparsnip, cow parsnip, masterwort.
