Characteristics:
Reproduces sexually or asexually by seed.
On the basis of cytological research, many former species are now included in Poa secunda. These include P. ampla, P. canbyi, P. gracillima, P. incurva, P. juncifolia, P. nevadensis, P. scabrella, and P. sandbergii (Larson et al 2001, Kellogg 1985, Kellogg 1987, Hitchcock et al 1969).
Because it grows and completes its life cycle early in the spring and senesces with the arrival of hot, dry weather, P. secunda is able to survive in areas which are too xeric even for bluebunch wheatgrass (Pseudoroegneria spicata).

The percent of apomictic seed set varies widely. Some seed may be produced sexually by outcrossing and self pollination is also possible (Kellogg 1987).
P. secunda is able to withstand heavy grazing and will persist when overgrazing has facilitated the invasion of annual grasses (Larson et al 2001).
Plants produce a set of leaves in the fall when rains begin. These remain on the plant thru winter and are replaced by a second set in the spring (Daubenmire 1972).
Poa species are hosts for larva of the juba skipper (Hesperia juba) and the garita skipper (Oarisma garita) butterflies. Many grasses are hosts for the larva of the common wood nymph (Cercyonis pegala) and the common ringlet (Coenonympha californica) butterflies (Pocewicz 2005).
The genus is an important source of livestock forage. Large ungulates and livestock graze the plants early in the growing season. Birds and small mammals use the seeds.

Other Propagation Information:
No pretreatment required (Rose et al 1998, Young & Young 1986).
Seed has a 6 month after-ripening period, but germinates at a wide range of temperatures when the after-ripening requirement is satisfied. Germination on the soil surface is minimal unless cover (mulch or litter) is provided. Planting seed deeper than 3 cm (1.2 inches) reduces germination and emergence (Evans et al 1977).
Optimum germination of Canbar (Poa canbyi) occurs at alternating temperatures of 15/20oC in the dark but high germination also occurs at lower temperatures (Young et al 1981).
Best germination of P. nevadensis occurs at alternating temperatures of 17/12oC (McDonough 1970).
Reproduces sexually or asexually by seed. Poa species generally grow readily from seed without pretreatment.
