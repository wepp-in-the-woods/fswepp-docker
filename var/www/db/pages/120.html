Plant Species: Aster conspicuus, showy aster

Kingdom: Plantae
Phylum: Magnoliophyta -- flowering plants
Class: Magnoliopsida -- dicots
Family: Asteraceae (Compositae) -- sunflower
Genus: Aster
Species: conspicuus
Variety:
Common Name: showy aster
Species Code: EUCO36 (ASCO3)
Origin: Native to moist meadows and open forest from Alaska south to Oregon and east to southern Manitoba and western South Dakota.
Rare: no

Form: forb, perennial, 30 cm - 100 cm tall, rhizomatous.
Duration: perennial
Longevity:
Habitat Type: forest
Wetland Indicator Status: not listed

Leaves: Alternate, sessile to clasping, ovate-elliptic, 6-8 cm long.
Mature height: 12-40 inches
Flowers: Rays blue or violet, 10-15 mm long.
Flower color: blue, purple
Bloom: July, August, September
Bloom starts on: July
Bloom ends on: September
Fruit: achene, fusiform, 3-4 mm long.
Vegetation type:
Characteristics:
Eurybia conspicua (Lindl.) G.L. Nesom is the currently accepted name.
Reproduces both sexually by seed and vegetatively by rhizomes. Flowering and seed production in mature stands is uncommon locally.
Fibrous rooted.
x=9 (University of British Columbia 2003).
2n=ca. 108, ca. 122 (Flora of North American Editorial Committee 1993).
Ray flowers are pistillate, disc flowers are perfect.
Fruit is an achene.
Seeds are dispersed by wind.
Aster species are hosts for the larva of the pearl crescent (Phyciodes tharos ssp. pascoensis) and the northern checkerspot (Charidryas palla) butterflies (Pocewicz 2005).
Comments:

Sun requirement: tolerates shade
Soil moisture: mesic
Precipitation:
Fire: Tops are killed but rhizomes survive moderate intensity fires and seedlings are recruited from the seedbank and from surrounding areas. Plants flower profusely the first few seasons following a fire (Reed 1993).
Aster conspicuus is moderately resistant to fire because the roots and rhizomes are deep enough below mineral soil to afford them some protection (McLean 1969).

Propagation:
Seeds need 30 days of cold moist stratification (McLean 1967).
Reproduces both sexually by seed and vegetatively by rhizomes. Can probably be propagated by divisions.
