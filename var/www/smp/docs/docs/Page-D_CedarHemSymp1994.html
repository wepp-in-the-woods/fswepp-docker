Relationships Among Woody Residues, Soil Organic Matter, and Ectomycorrhizae in the Cedar-Hemlock Ecosystem
Deborah Page-Dumroese, Martin Jurgensen, and Alan Harvey

ABSTRACT

Ectomycorrhizal colonization of conifer species associated with a cedar-hemlock (Thuja plicata Donn ex D. Don and Tsuga heterophylla [Raf. ] Sarg. ) forest in northern Idaho, is directly influenced by the quantity and type of soil organic matter. Soil organic horizons, particularly soil wood, represent the substrate for a majority of the ectomycorrhizal activity. The amount of large downed woody residue ranges from 38 Mg ha−1 to 202 Mg ha−1. Residues play an important role in nutrient cycling and moisture storage capacity. Surface mineral horizons contain significant quantities of volcanic ash and organic matter, which also helps cycle nitrogen and maintain moisture. The critical nature of soil organics in this ecosystem represents hazards, and opportunities for forest managers. With careful management, these soils can be protected, or even improved. Recognition of the importance of organic and ash horizons also provides a basis for restoring damaged soils, soils that are impossible to restore, or for soils requiring long restoration periods.

Keywords: Organic horizons, ectomycorrhizal colonization, soil productivity, volcanic ash

INTRODUCTION

The most productive forests in northern Idaho are usually the cedar-hemlock (Thuju plicata Donn ex D. Don and Tsuga heterophylla [Raf.] Sarg.) habitat types (Cooper et al. 1991). High productivity is attributed to a relatively warm, moist climate, high organic matter content, fairly deep forest floor, and thick ash-cap soil (Jurgensen et al. 1990; Page-Dumroese et al. 1991).

Soils characteristic of this ecosystem are usually Inceptisols, Andisols, or Alfisols (Meurisse et al. 1991) and are largely influenced by the volcanic ash-cap deposits. Surface organic horizons help maintain moisture in the mineral horizons (Harvey et al. 1987). Buried organic matter, particularly decayed wood, can improve aeration, structure, infiltration, percolation, and moisture retention, while decreasing compaction (Lull 1959). These horizons are critical for providing tree-available moisture, and nitrogen (N), and are important for ectomycorrhizal development. As a result, total tree harvesting, extensive woody residue removal, and surface organic matter displacement during timber harvesting operations can impact soil processes and productivity (Harvey et al. 1976a, 1980; Jurgensen et al. 1979).

Disturbance of these soils during timber harvest usually removes surface organic matter (Niehoff 1985; Nielson-Gerhardt 1986), and reduces its residence time through mixing with surface mineral soil (Geist and Cochran 1991). Nitrogen availability from severely disturbed sites can be reduced by 50% over that available in undisturbed stands (Nielson-Gerhardt 1986). In addition, ash-cap thickness is usually cut in half through displacement or compression (Geist and Cochran 1991).

Most studies concentrating on timber harvesting have highlighted the contribution of organic matter to nutrient availability and cycling (Powers et al. 1990; Stark 1980). While soil nutrients are a predominant concern in the cedar-hemlock ecosystem, organic matter also influences soil physical properties, such as water-holding capacity and aeration (Page-Dumroese 1993). Changes in these properties may be just as important as nutrient losses for subsequent stand growth (Jurgensen et al. 1990). Consequently, a study was established as part of the national Long-Term Soil Productivity Study (Powers et al. 1990) to determine the influence of compaction and organic matter removal on site productivity. This paper discusses preharvest organic matter content and its function with regard to support of ectomycorrhizal activity on a cedar-hemlock site.

SITE DESCRIPTION

The study site is located at the Priest River Experimental Forest in northern Idaho. It is at an elevation of 715 m on a flat bench adjoining the Priest River. The soil is a silt loam, medial, frigid Ochreptic Fragixeralf underlain at depths of 30–96 cm by gravelly to very gravelly sands and sandy loam deposited by alluvial processes. Soil surface horizons consist of about 3 cm of forest floor organics and 28–38 cm of volcanic ash derived from Mt. Mazama (Fryzell 1965). Habitat type is Tsuga hererophyllal Clintonia uniflora (Cooper et al. 1991). Annual precipitation averages 84 cm, with a mean annual temperature of 6.6°C (Wellner 1976). The overstory consisted of a well-stocked stand of ca 120-year-old hemlock, western white pine (Pinus monticola Dougl. ex D. Don), western redcedar, Douglas-fir (Pseudotsugu menziesii var. glauca Beissn.] Franco), and western larch (Larix occidentalis Nutt.). In the past, this site was part of the Priest River Experimental Forest Arboretum. Approximately 40 years ago some selective harvesting was done, creating one skid trail that was avoided during treatment layout.

METHODS

This site was divided into nine 0.8 ha plots each surrounded by a 200 m buffer. Core samples (10 x 30 cm) were taken in each plot at 16 locations in late May and early June 1991. Soil horizon depth was measured, then each core was divided into litter (Oi), duff (Oe), humus (Oa), charcoal, soil wood, surface mineral soil (0–10 cm deep), deep mineral soil (10–30 cm deep or to the contact with gravel), and surface decayed wood. Soil wood was identified as buried within the soil profile; surface decayed wood was above the mineral horizons and horizontal with the soil surface. In the laboratory, each sample was weighed, and moisture and rock content were determined. Ectomycorrhizal root tips were counted with a 20x binocular microscope on all the woody roots removed from each core horizon (Harvey et al. 1976b). Five downed wood transects were taken in each plot to determine the amount of large downed wood (Brown 1974). Soil horizons were combined to yield four samples per plot.

Soil samples were passed through a 2 mm sieve and dried at 105°C before analysis. Organic matter was determined by weight loss after combustion at 375°C for 16 h (Ball 1964). Potential mineralizable N was estimated using the anaerobic incubation technique on undried samples (Powers 1980). Nitrate (NO3−) and ammonium (NH4+) were determined from a 1N KC1 extract using an Alpkem Rapid Flow Analyzer1 (Kenney and Nelson 1982). Soil field capacity and permanent wilting point were determined using pressure plates (Cassel and Nielson 1986).

The significance of differences between soil horizons was tested by one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) for a randomized complete block design followed by Scheffe's multiple range test (Scheffe 1953). The accepted level of significance was p < 0.05.

RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

Organic horizons on this site comprised almost 33% of the soil profile volume to a depth of 30 cm (Table 1). This is substantially more than the 15 found by Harvey et al. (1987) on sites in western Montana. However, this site is similar to the cedar-hemlock site described by Jurgensen et al. (1990). Studies indicate that woody materials contained within organic horizons have an important role in carbon cycling, nutrient storage, stream ecology, and erosion control (Maser and Trappe 1984; Harmon et al. 1986; Jurgensen et al. 1987; Sollins et al. 1987).

Large amounts of organic matter translate into increased soil water-holding capacity. The large volume of deep mineral soil can hold more available moisture, but very few roots are located in this horizon (Kimmins and Hawkes 1978). Surface decayed wood, although substantially shallower than the surface mineral horizon, held similar amounts of available moisture. Surface decayed wood is a valuable source of moisture throughout the growing season and is critical both for the growth of established trees and successful reforestation (Harvey et al. 1987).

Much of the organic matter was found in surface horizons (Table 2). In addition, this site averaged 120 Mg/ha of large woody residue. The percent of organic matter content in the mineral horizons was also high compared to other soil types in this region (Jurgensen et al. 1990). Mineral soil derived from other parent materials in this region contains significantly less organic matter (Geist and Cochran 1991). Volcanic ash-cap soils are rich in organic components that contribute a great deal to moisture-holding capacity and nutrient cycling. On this site, litter, humus, soil wood, and surface decayed wood horizons are also the locations of the greatest N mineralization. Soil wood is the substrate with the greatest NO3− and NH4+ content. Other ecosystem studies have documented the importance of organic horizons to microbially-mediated processes, such as mineralization and nitrification (e.g., Woods and Schuman 1986). Plant growth, organic matter decomposition, and nutrient cycling require that moderate quantities of available nutrients accumulate and persist in the soil (Anderson 1977). Without adequate pools of available nutrients, long-term fertility problems can arise, even if plant growth is initially satisfactory (Schuman and Power 1981).

Ectomycorrhizal root colonization is strongly related to soil productivity on many sites in the Intermountain West (Harvey et al. 1979). Studies in old-growth stands in this region have shown a strong correlation between ectomycorrhizal root tips and organic horizons (Jurgensen et al. 1990). The surface mineral soil and organic horizons are the zones of elevated fine root numbers (Kimmins and Hawkes 1978; Harvey et al. 1986). This trend is also evident on this cedar-hemlock site (Table 3). Even though soil wood comprised only 10% of the surface soil volume, it was the site of 65% of the active ectomycorrhizal tips, presumably because of its capacity to hold moisture. In periods of adequate soil moisture, forest floor horizons can support high numbers of ectomycorrhizae, but during dry summer conditions soil wood is more likely to support high numbers of ectomycorrhizae (Harvey et al. 1978). This is because soil wood is less subject to daily and seasonal variations in temperature and moisture than other shallow surface horizons (Hungerford 1980).

MANAGEMENT CONSIDERATIONS

The fundamental ties between soil N, ectomycorrhizal colonization, and soil productivity are the organic matter reserves and parent materials from which organic horizons are derived (Flinn et al. 1980; Harvey et al. 1987). Adequate ectomycorrhizal root tip development is critical to successful seedling establishment after timber harvesting throughout the Intermountain West (Perry et al. 1987; Harvey et al. 1991). Reduced ectomycorrhizal formation after clearcutting, particularly after fires, may affect seedling growth and survival (Harvey et al. 1980; Perry et al. 1982). Most natural regeneration found in old-growth stands in the interior Rocky Mountains becomes established where the surface organic horizons are deeper than average (Harvey et al. 1978). Ponderosa pine habitat types are an exception.

Most cedar-hemlock stands in the Intermountain West are considered to have adequate organic matter reserves and are not considered at risk of organic matter depletion following careful harvesting. Enough soil organic matter usually remains after harvest to maintain a site. However, extensive site preparation that displaces and mixes organic horizons with the surface ash-cap or mineral soil over large areas can have significant impacts on productivity (Jurgensen et al. 1990). In particular, loss of the surface ash-cap or mineral soil and organic horizons through slash removal or compaction increases by more than 20% in bulk density (Page-Dumroese 1993), has been linked to substantial declines in seedling growth (Froehlich et al. 1985), and has the potential to reduce long-term productivity (Jurgensen et al. 1990). On most cedar-hemlock sites slash and downed wood create little danger of wildfire (see Neuenschwander and Shiplett, these proceedings). Therefore, careful fire prescriptions and other site preparation methods can result in minimal impact on both organic reserves and ectomycorrhizae and all reasonable precautions should be taken to reduce or prevent compaction associated with any treatment.
