SOILS REPORT CHECKERBOARD LAND EXCHANGE

LOUIS J. KUENNEN

I. FOREST PLAN REQUIREMENTS

The soil resource is the key ingredient for maintaining the productive

potential of an area. Maintenance of soil quality is dependent on the

protection of the surface soils from erosion, displacement, and compaction and

the continued input of organic matter to the soil.

The Forest Plan requires that project plans, for activities requiring use of

ground-based equipment, establish standards for the area allocated to skid

trails, landings, temporary roads or similar areas of concentrated use. Best

Management Practices constitute the state-of-the-art in prudent practices to

protect soil productivity and water quality. The standard used should minimize

the area of impact to the extent practical. The Forest Plan also requires that

practices such as dozer piling or mechanical site preparation not be planned

without considering the feasibility of limiting soil conditions under which

equipment operates or alternative practices which do not require ground-based

equipment.

Since the Kootenai National Forest Plan became final, the Forest Service has

established policy relative to management effects on soil productivity (FSH

2509.18). This policy establishes a guideline that soil productivity be

reduced by no more than fifteen (15) percent. It also requires Regional

Foresters to establish standards for detrimental changes in soil properties

that will comply with this guideline. A Region 1 supplement to FSH 2509.18

proposes standards based on monitoring results and professional judgement.

This standard requires that no more than fifteen (15) percent of an activity

area have significantly impaired productivity due to displacement of soil

surface layers. The total area affected by displacement, compaction and

puddling cannot exceed 30 percent of the area treated.

The National Forest Management Act requires that soil productivity be

maintained or enhanced.

II. GENERAL DESCRIPTION

Glacial activity had the last major effect on shaping the landscape. Prior to

glaciation water erosion was the major land forming feature.

All eight of the areas within the land exchange have been glaciated. The areas

further north were closer to the source of ice and were more strongly affected

by glacial activity. Portions of the Jumbo and Blacktail Peak areas have some

land that was minimally affected. Some of the area around Allen Peak was

affected by an ice cap. The glacial activity did a lot of scraping and

scouring and filling creating a more subdued landscape than would have existed

prior to glaciation. Generally major ridge divides and smaller ridgetops were

scoured leaving exposed scraped rock. The scoured soil material was pushed

around and tended to fill in topographic lows (drainage bottoms, etc.). The

scouring of the ridgetops and filling of drainage bottoms gave a rounded

appearance.

Glacial ice generally retreated from the area 12,000 to 15,000 years ago. The

soil material left was composed of silts and sands (glacial flour) and rounded

gravels and boulders. As the ice melted more landforms were created. Most of

these landforms were in and/or adjacent to the drainage bottoms. The landforms

consist of outwash terraces, eskers, kames, and lacustrine terraces.

Approximately 6800 years ago a volcanic eruption in the Cascades deposited a

layer of volcanic ash-influenced loess over northwestern Montana on top of the

existing landscape. This layer now exists on all northerly and easterly

aspects and on the southerly and westerly aspects at the higher elevations

(generally above 4500 feet). This layer is light and fluffy and has a brownish

color.

The eight areas within the exchange have had a soil survey (Appendix A)

completed for them. The survey is referred to as the Kootenai National Forest

Area Land System Inventory (1984). The map units are referred to landtypes.

Each landtype contains specific landform, soil, vegetative, and geologic

components. There are five major groupings of landtypes--water-influenced

topography (100s), breaklands (200s), continentally glaciated lands (300s),

alpine glaciated landforms (400s), and erosional lands (500s). These are

described in Appendix B.

All parts of the landscape--stream bottoms, mountain slopes and ridgetops--are

represented within the exchange. These are represented by the range of

landtypes, the descriptions of which are given in Appendix C. The

interpretations of each landtype are given in Appendix D.

III. COMMON EFFECTS

Timber harvest can affect soil productivity in the following ways:

Soil Compaction

Soil surface layers are compacted by operation of ground-based equipment

used for logging and brush piling (Steinbrenner 1955). Compaction

significantly lowers soil productivity by reducing root penetration and

permeability. Compaction is most severe when ground-based equipment

operates on moist soil or makes repeated trips over the same area. The

soils on the Kootenai National Forest are particularly sensitive to

compaction because most have surface layers formed in volcanic

ash-influenced loess. The loess has relatively low natural bulk densities

of 0.65 to 0.9 grams per cubic centimeter and, therefore, low strength.

A moist soil is more susceptible to compaction than a dry soil. As a soil

becomes wetter, though, it can even lose its bearing capacity. When this

occurs and machinery operates on the soil, the soil can no longer support

heavy weight that is placed on it and the soil loses its structure. The

loose, granular structure that is good for growing plants is destroyed.

This "puddled" soil does not support very good tree growth.

Many of the soils on the Kootenai Forest are susceptible to soil

compaction. Included are all the soils with a volcanic ash surface layer,

those forming in glacial till and those forming in lacustrine materials.

The amount of soil compacted as a result of timber harvest operations is

directly related to the amount of ground skidding. The problem is further

enhanced on those lands where machinery is used for treatment of fuels.

Natural processes restore compacted layers to normal density slowly and the

compactive effects can last for as long (or longer) as it takes to grow

another crop of trees. Longevity has not been determined because those

studies that have dealt with the compaction issue have not seen the effects

completely ameliorated. Extensive freezing and thawing and to a lesser

extent wetting and drying are the main factors that overcome compaction. A

lot of the precipitation that falls on the Kootenai Forest comes in the

form of snow. Snow is a good insulator and generally prevents freezing

(Ford 1986). Following logging, many of the sites in the Decision Area do

not dry out. In this case, the main factors for naturally reducing

compaction have been negated.

Soil Erosion

Soil erosion occurs when the energy from flowing water is greater than the

ability of the soil to resist deformation. As the water concentrates and

increases its speed, enough energy is generated to detach soil particles,

beginning the erosion process. The places most susceptible are skid

trails, landings, firelines, and roads. Sometimes sufficient flow is

generated during storm events or below culverts that erosion (occasionally

debris flows) occurs without removal of the organic mat. Erosion of the

soil surface removes the most important part of the soil. Since the soils

are young (not highly developed), most of the nutrients are stored in the

organic matter and the top several inches of soil. Loss of organics and/or

soil mass through erosion can have a strong affect on site quality.

Soil Displacement

Soil surface layers are displaced during dozer piling and on logging skid

trails, landings, firelines, and temporary roads. Surface layers contain

most of the soil fertility. Productivity is significantly impaired when

the surface layer is removed.

Nitrogen Depletion

Nitrogen, a very important soil nutrient, is lost by removal of organic

matter from the site. Logs are removed taking some nitrogen from the

site. Slash burning volatilizes nitrogen and returns it to the

atmosphere. In addition, removal of the forest canopy exposes the soil to

direct sunlight and warms it (Harvey 1981a). Mineralization (decomposition

of the organic matter into its component parts, such as nitrogen) of

remaining nitrogen is accelerated by warmer soil temperatures (Jurgensen et

al. 1979).

Highly mobile, mineralized nitrogen can be lost through leaching. Loss of

nitrogen through normal fire and timber harvesting, though, is not expected

to measurably affect long-term soil productivity. In a natural

environment, annual mineralization is much greater than annual losses. In

a disturbed forest, mineralization rates are artificially increased beyond

the need of plant uptake. This excess N, in the form of ammonium created

in the mineralization process, can be quickly converted to nitrates and

lost by leaching from the system (Dominski 1971; Powers 1982; Vitousek and

Mellilo 1979). Complete removal of soil organic matter in the form of

debris, litter, humus and surface soil can rob the site of mineralizable N

reserves. Minimal amounts of organic reserves left on a site may be

quickly depleted due to increased mineralization and nitrification

(Nielsen-Gerhardt 1986).

The breakdown of organic matter is the source of future nutrients.

Maintaining a fairly uniform level of organic matter in all stages of decay

is very desirable for sustaining site quality. At least fifteen to

twenty-five (15-25) tons of larger than four (4") inches woody debris

should be kept on site to maintain site quality (Harvey 1982). All action

alternatives would accomplish this.

Overland Flow

In those areas where there are high amounts of precipitation and soil

disturbance, overland flow is likely to occur. The results could be a

significant reduction in water quality. Research has indicated that

increased soil-water content in harvested areas may cause early fall or

initial snowmelt to produce more runoff than under forested conditions

(Chamberlin 1982). These increases in soil-water content and ground-water

levels following harvest have been demonstrated to be an important factor

in increasing the rate of slope mass movements after harvesting (Chamberlin

1982). Because the soil in forested openings is wetter (closer to its

storage capacity), not only is more water available to become streamflow

but it becomes available earlier causing higher peak flows (USDA Forest

Service 1982; Rice 1980; Rainville 1987).

IV. ASSUMPTIONS

Monitoring on the Kootenai National Forest and adjacent Flathead and Kaniksu

National Forests indicates the following assumptions can be reasonably made

when predicting the extent of detrimental effects from timber harvest on soil

productivity in the Decision Area.

1) In road construction, four (4) acres per mile are disturbed and will be

lost for crop production.

2) Logging with cable equipment will moderately affect ten (10) percent of

a logging unit.

3) Logging with ground-based equipment will affect soil productivity:

a) past logging activity affected soil productivity on thirty (30)

percent of a logging unit--fifteen (15) percent severely and fifteen

(15) percent moderately; and

b) current logging activity affects soil productivity on twenty two

and one-half (22.5) percent of a logging unit--seven and one-half

(7.5) percent severely and fifteen (15) percent moderately.

4) In addition, hazard abatement with dozers will severely affect seven

and one half (7.5) percent of the ground through displacement and

compaction and twenty (20) percent will be moderately impacted. In

contrast, the use of an excavator with grapples will reduce the amount of

area moderately impacted by five (5) percent and totally eliminate the

amount severely effected.

5) Those areas taken out of production--system roads, temporary

roads--will be lost to future production. Those areas severely impacted by

compaction, surface disturbance, overland flow, loss of organic matter, and

erosion are estimated to have a twenty-five to seventy-five (25-75) percent

(generally closer to the higher level) loss in growth potential over the

next rotation. The areas that will be moderately impacted are estimated to

have a zero to twenty-five (0-25) percent loss in growth potential.

CUMULATIVE EFFECTS

The cumulative effects of the proposed alternatives would include those of all

the proposed actions as well as those that have already occurred.

Twenty-five to seventy-five (25-75) percent crop production will be lost on the

heavily impacted areas over the next rotation. Zero to twenty-five (0-25)

percent crop production will be lost on the moderately impacted areas.

CONSISTENCY WITH FOREST PLAN

1. Units cable logged and broadcast burned will easily meet the proposed

standards for maintenance of soil productivity.

2. Units logged with ground-based equipment and broadcast burned will barely

meet the standards for maintenance of soil productivity. If dedicated trails

are used, the soil compaction/soil displacement is reduced. They are predicted

to have seven and one half (7.5) percent of the area allocated to skid trails,

landings, and temporary roads which are likely to have significantly impaired

productivity due to topsoil displacement and compaction.

3. Units that are logged with ground-based equipment and hazard abated with

dozers/rubber-tired skidders will not meet standards.

If Soil and Water practices (Soil and Water Conservation Practices Handbook) are

incorporated in all land use and project planning and are carried out in all

activities, the alternatives are consistent with the Forest Plan.
