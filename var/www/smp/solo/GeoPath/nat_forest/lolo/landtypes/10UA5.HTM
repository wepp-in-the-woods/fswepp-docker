MAP UNIT DESCRIPTION

M.U. 10UA Fluvents, Ochrepts, and Aquepts, stream bottoms

MAP UNIT SUMMARY

This map unit occurs in stream bottoms. Soils are formed in a complex of well

drained and poorly drained alluvial deposits. Native vegetation is a warm

forested riparian community type.

TOPOGRAPHY

This map unit consists of recent floodplains, perennial streams, and low stream

terraces 2- 10 feet above the existing stream channel. Floodplains may have

braided stream channels that change course frequently. Floodplains and terraces

have fluctuating water tables. The lowest portions of these floodplains flood

annually. Other terraces may flood every 25 years or so.

VEGETATION

COMPOSITION The map unit contains a mosaic of habitat types and vegetation

communities that is determined by a complex pattern of well drained and poorly

drained soils. Douglas fir series HTs and grassy meadow are on well drained

sites. The poorly drained and frequently flooded soils are occupied by wet sedge

meadows and stands of cottonwood. A relatively warm climate and moderate timber

productivity is associated with this map unit. Seasonal cool air drainage may

occur.

Included are up to 5 percent dissimilar HTs. Subalpine fir/queencup beadlily

(ABLA/CLUN), subalpine fir/common horsetail (ABLA/EQAR), and western

redcedar/queencup beadlily (THPL/CLUN) can occur in the upper elevation bounds

of this unit. These HTs are colder and receive more precipitation.

EXISTING VEGETATION is ponderosa pine and Douglas fir on the well drained stream

terraces and cottonwood on the poorly draines soils and floodplains. The forest

understory is composed of kinnikinnick, pinegrass, snowberry, and Idaho

GEOLOGY

The unit is underlain by alluvial deposits. The alluvium consists of stratified

sands, rounded gravels, and silts. These alluvial deposits can be very thick,

textures are usually stratified and are quite variable. The alluvial gravels are

derived primarily by argillites, siltites, and quartzites from the Belt

supergroup.

SOILS

COMPOSITION This map unit is a mosaic of well drained and poorly drained soils

depending on height of rise of water tables and proximity to stream course.

MANAGEMENT IMPLICATIONS

TIMBER Timber productivity is moderate. Productivity is dependent on the finer

alluvial soils because the coarser alluvial soils can be infertile. Slopes

provide for tractor harvest. Soil displacement will reduce site productivity

where a silty surface is removed and the coarse textured subsoil is exposed.

ROADS Shallow ground water and seasonal flooding can limit construction and

increase maintenance. Turnpiking can be used to overcome problems with wet

subgrades. Flooding and stream channel changes can damage bridges or culverts.

The small areas of deep silty alluvium have low bearing strength and will rut.

Rocky subsoils and substrata produce rough road surfaces. Surfacing improves the

quality of the road.

RANGE This map unit is moderately suited to range management. Potential forage

production is high. This map unit contains the highly sensitive riparian areas.

Steep sideslopes in adjacent map units force cattle to stay in these riparian

bottoms which creates problems with overgrazing sensitive riparian vegetation

and compaction of wet soils.

WILDLIFE-FISHERIES Protection from sediment production in streams is critical

for high quality fisheries. ADDITIONAL COMMENTS TO BE OBTAINED BY BIOLOGISTS.

WATERSHED The major watershed management concern is controlling sediment

produced when an activity causing soil disturbance occurs adjacent to the

stream. Proximity of streams to any disturbance in this map unit will be less

than 200 feet. Stream channel changes can produce high amounts of sediment.

Design of activities away from streamside and from altering natural stream

courses will promote streambank protection.
