MAP UNIT DESCRIPTION

M.U. 26.Ua

MAP UNIT SUMMARY

M.U. SETTING The landform consists of steep, craggy breaks. Rock outcrops and talus are major components of the unit. This map unit occurs along the

Clark Fork River and several major order streams.

M.U. COMPONENTS Soils are excessively drained, very shallow, and rocky. Soils comprise less than 50% of the unit with the remaining portion being comprised of bedrock outcrop and talus. Bedrock is comprised of the Belt Supergroup. Native vegetation is sparse Douglas fir forest.

ADJOINING M.U. Adjacent units are very steep, straight mountain slopes that drain immediately into major streams and alluvial terraces at the toeslope of this unit.

This map unit contains steep, very rocky mountain slopes. Craggy breaks, rock outcrop, and large deposits of talus occur in this unit. Slopes vary with orientation of bedrock sometimes forming shallow benches between outcrops. Large stringers of talus between outcrops are common. Sediment delivery effeciency potential is high due to steepness of slope and close proximity to live streams, although, sources of fine sediment are few in this unit.

VEGETATION

HABITAT TYPE (HT) OCCURRENCE The unit consists of sparse stands and pockets

of forest vegetation. Habitat types include Douglas fir/ninebark (PSME/PHMA), Douglas fir/snowberry (PSME/SYAL), Douglas fir/pinegrass (PSME/CARU), and Douglas fir/Idaho fescue (PSME/FEID).

HABITAT TYPE DISTRIBUTION PSME/PHMA AND PSME/SYAL occur as sparse stands throughout the unit wherever bedrock does not restrict vegetation. PSME/CARU and PSME/FEID occur on benches and pockets of deeper soils.

Included are up to 5 percent dissimilar HTs. The dissimilar included HTs are PSME/AGSP on shallow soils and southerly inclusions.

Rocky, very shallow soils severely limit productivity in this unit. Regeneration is very difficult. Harvest suitability is poor in this map unit due to sparseness of stands, low productivity, and very low success in regeneration of stands.

EXISTING VEGETATION consists of sparse stands of Douglas fir forests with occasional pockets of denser, more productive Douglas fir forests. Seral stands include Ponderosa pine and western larch. The forest understory is composed of dense stands of shrubs, dominated by ninebark or snowberry.Understory vegetation also includes pinegrass, bluebunch wheatgrass, spirea, pinegrass, serviceberry, and chokecherry.

Open stands of ninebark and snowberry occur without a conifer overstory in rubble stringers. Bluebunch wheatgrass and Idaho fescue occur on extremely shallow soils at the interface of bedrock outcrops.

GEOLOGY

OCCURRENCE The unit is underlain by Belt supergroup metasedimentary bedrock. The bedrock consists of thick to thin beds of argillites, siltites, and quartzites. Bedrock consistency varies by Belt Supergroup Formation.

Included with dissimilar properties are pockets of lacustrine or deltaic deposits occuring in some draws or benches. This material occupies less than 5% of the unit. These deposits offer minor inclusions of deeper soils within a map unit predominately of rock outcrop. These inclusions can be identified by change in stand density and productivity.

SOILS

GENERAL NATURE OF SOILS Soils are excessively drained, extremely rocky, and very shallow. Soils are moderately coarse textured and contain 60-95% rock fragments.

OCCURRENCE AND DISTRIBUTION The map unit is a complex of soils and rock outcroppings. Rock rubble, talus, and rock outcrop comprise >50% of the map unit. Very shallow, rocky soils comprise 45% of the unit. Soils occur on benches and between rock outcrops.

Small areas of dissimilar soils are included. These soils are deeper and occur on some benches and pockets of lacustrine or deltaic deposits. They comprise 5% of the map unit. These soils have less rock fragments (35%-50%) and are well drained. They have medium textures and are somewhat more productive.

GENERAL DESCRIPTION Surface layers have a brown to dark brown very gravelly sandy loam about 3 inches thick. The subsoil is an extremely cobbly extremely stony brown sandy loam ranging from 10 to 17 inches thick. The substratum is an extremely stony to fragmental sandy loam ranging from 0 to 3 inches thick. Depth to bedrock ranges 1-3 feet. Soil description ___ is representative of the soils in this map unit.

CLASSIFICATION REMARKS The major soils are Lithic Ustochrepts, loamy skeletal, mixed, frigid. Included similar soils where bedrock is within 2 feet are Udic Ustochrepts, loamy skeletal, mixed, frigid.

The included dissimilar soils are Udic Ustochrepts, loamy skeletal, mixed, frigid where soils are 3 feet deep or deeper over bedrock.

MANAGEMENT IMPLICATIONS

TIMBER This map unit is poorly suited to timber management. Potential annual productivity is ______ cu.ft/acre/yr or very low. Slope steepness and rock outcrop limit equipment operation. Regeneration is limited by droughtiness, talus, and bedrock outcrops.

ROADS This map unit is poorly suited to road construction. Slope steepness increases the quantity of material excavated. Large amounts of bedrock must be excavated, much of which is non-rippable. Talus stringers and extremely rocky soils make native road surfaces very rough and tire tread wear is high. Rock fall from high cutbanks will be a maintenance problem.

RANGE This map unit is poorly suited to range management. Rock, rubble, and steepness of slope limit access and mobility. Minor inclusions may be offer remote areas for grazing. Forage consists of pinegrass, bluebunch wheatgrass, Idaho fescue, serviceberry, and chokecherry.

WILDLIFE-FISHERIES This map unit offers potential bighorn sheep and mountain goat habitat. Areas along the Clark Fork River between St. Regis and Thompson Falls support bighorn sheep, presently. Forage values are moderate on most slopes and high on benches that have deeper soils.

WATERSHED The major watershed management concern is increased sediment yield due to road construction. Sediment delivery efficiency is high but in most cases, erodibility of soils is low due to high rock content.

VISUALS Units along the Clark Fork River and major order streams are within retention. Visual values are difficult to maintain because of steep slopes which require extensive excavation during road construction and sparse forest cover.
