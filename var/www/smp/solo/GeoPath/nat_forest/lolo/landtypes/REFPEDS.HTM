REFERENCE PEDON. Colors are for moist soil unless otherwise indicated.

A1 0-6 ins. - Dark brown (7.5 YR 3/1) silt loam, dark brown (10 YR 3/3) dry;

moderate medium granular and subangular blocky structure; nonsticky and

nonplastic; many fine and coarse, few medium roots; 7 percent gravel;

moderately acid; clear smooth boundary.

A2 6-14 ins. - Dark brown (7.5 YR 3/2) silt loam, yellowish brown (10 YR

5/5) dry; moderate fine and medium subangular blocky structure; nonsticky

and slightly plastic; few coarse and common fine and medium roots; 7

percent gravel; slightly acid; abrupt broken boundary.

BwA 14-32 ins. - Dark yellowish brown (10 YR 4/4) very cobbly silt loam,

light yellowish brown (10 YR 6/4) dry; moderate medium to strong fine

subangular blocky structure; slightly sticky and slightly plastic; few

medium and fine roots; 15 percent gravel; 22 percent cobble; neutral;

clear smooth boundary.

C 32-48+ ins. - Yellowish brown (10 YR 5/6) very gravelly silt loam, yellow

(10 YR 7/6) dry; massive structure; slightly sticky and slightly plastic;

no roots; 27 percent gravel; 12 percent cobble; neutral.

LOCATION AND SETTING: West-central Montana; Bitterroot Mountains; Elizabeth

Gulch; NW1/4 SW1/4 section 7, T. 15 N., R. 27 W., map unit 43.Sb. This soil is

formed in a 14-inch deep volcanic ash mantle over moderately weathered Belt

argillite. The slopes gradient is 10 percent on an east aspect at 5,400 feet

elevation. The community type is a moist forest opening dominated by broken

fern, western coneflower, and sedges with scattered spruce and subalpine fir.

The ranges of selected characteristics for Andic Cryumbrepts are shown below.

These soils are formed in a 10- to 14-inch deep volcanic ash mantle over varied

substratum. Subsurface textures range from silt loam in soft Belt sedimentary

bedrock to sandy loam in granitic bedrock. Organic horizons have formed in the

ash mantle of these soils. Rock fragments increase in volume and size from

surface to subsurface horizons.

Andic Cryumbrept, loamy-skeletal are like the reference pedon. These soils

contain over 35 percent rock fragment throughout most of the profile with a

loamy fine material matrix.

The ranges of characteristics for Andeptic Cryoboralfs are shown below. These

soils can be associated with several parent materials but are associated

primarily with glacial land forms. Most soils are in fine-loamy or

loamy-skeletal families. Eluvial surface horizons are developed in an 8- to

12-inch deep ash mantle. Fine families have 0 to 15 percent gravel throughout

the profile. Whereas, skeletal families contain 35 to 60+ percent gravel.

Andeptic Cryoboralf, loamy-skeletal are like the reference pedon. They have 8-

to 12-inch deep silt loam surface horizons composed of volcanic ash. Subsoils

and substrata contain more than 35 percent rock fragments. Clayey subsurface

textures are associated with soft sedimentary parent material. Whereas, loamy

textures are formed from hard belt metasedimentary parent material.

Andeptic Cryoboralf, fine-loamy differ from the parent material by having less

than 35 percent rock fragments throughout the profile.

-6-

was formed in soft sedimentary glacial till with a 10-inch deep volcanic ash

The range of selected characteristics is shown below. Typic Eutroboralfs can be

formed from several parent materials. However, the primary parent material is

lacustrine valley fill derived from Lake Missoula silts. The ash mantle varies

from nonexistent to 12-inch deep on these soils. Surface horizons are extremely

variable. Shallow organic, eluvial, or ash horizons are all present. The major

family is loamy-skeletal with fine-loamy, clayey-skeletal, and fine-silty

representation. The valley fill parent material produces primarily silty

clay-loam to sandy-clay-loam subsurface textures.

The soils formed in soft sedimentary continental till have silty or sandy clay

subsurface textures.

(Joni-by rough estimate frag families seem to occur on southerly slopes--the

fine families on northerly and westerly slopes.)

Typic Eutroboralfs, loamy-skeletal are similar to the reference pedon. These

soils have greater than 35 percent rock fragments throughout the profile. They

are formed in undifferentiated, valley fill, and glacial till parent material.

Typic Eutroboralfs, fine-loamy are different from the reference pedon by having

less than 35 percent rock fragments throughout the profile. These soils are

formed primarily in valley fill parent material but at a slightly higher

elevation than fine-silty soils.

Typic Eutroboralfs, clayey-skeletal are different from the reference pedon by

having clayey subsurface textures instead of loamy. These soils are mostly

formed in soft sedimentary continental till.

Typic Eutroboralfs, fine-silty are different from the reference pedon by having

less than 35 percent rock fragments throughout the profile and silt instead of

loamy subsoil textures. These soils are formed primarily in silty valley fill

parent material.

The ranges of selected characteristics for Mollic Haploxeralfs are shown below.

Mollic Haploxeralfs are formed in moderately weathered (siltite and argillite)

metasedimentary substratum. They occur on moderate mountain sideslopes. Low

elevation soils have finer and more silty textures than the higher elevation and

-9-

steeper slope soils. This reflects an inclusion of soils formed in glacial lake

siltite? Finer textures are also found near fault lines where they occur. Most

soils are in the loamy-skeletal family. (All occur on S to SE slopes.)

Mollic Haploxeralf, fine-silty occur at lower elevations and are more weathered

than the skeletal family. They have a silty subsoil texture and less than 15

percent throughout the profile.

Mollic Haploxeralf, loamy-skeletal occur on steeper more eroded slopes at higher

elevations than the non-skeletal families. Coarse fragments range from 45 to 55

percent through the profile. Their subsurface texture is loamy. The reference

pedon differs from the family as it contains 10 to 35 percent gravel and is

classified into the fine-loamy family.

The ranges of selected characteristics for lithic cryandepts are shown below.

These soils are associated with hard Belt metasedimentary parent material. The

surface horizons are consistently deeper than subsurface horizons. They are

formed in a 6 to 16 inch deep volcanic ash mantle. In many cases, the ash

horizon composes the entire soil mantle overlying slightly weathered bedrock.

These soils occur on glacially scoured landforms. Shallow organic horizons have

developed in most of these soils.

TYPIC XERORTHENT

REFERENCE PEDON: Colors are for moist soils unless otherwise indicated.

O Trace of duff; mostly charcoal.

A 0-4 ins. - Very dark gray (10 YR 3/1) sandy loam, light gray (10 YR 7/2)

dry; moderate fine subangular blocky structure; friable, nonsticky and

nonplastic; few medium and common fine roots; 13 percent gravel; neutral;

abrupt smooth boundary.

C1 4-8 ins. - Brown (10 YR 5/3) sandy loam, very pale brown (10 YR 7/3) dry;

weak fine subangular blocky structure; loose, nonsticky and nonplastic;

few fine roots; 13 percent gravel; neutral; clear smooth boundary.

C2 8-20 ins. - Yellowish brown (10 YR 5/4) extremely gravelly loamy sand,

light yellowish brown (10 YR 6/4) dry; single grained structures loose,

nonsticky and nonplastic; common fine and many coarse roots; 65 percent

gravel; slightly acid; clear smooth boundary.

C3 20-30 ins. - Variable colors, extremely gravelly, single grained

structures loose; nonsticky and nonplastic; few very fine roots; 65

percent gravel; slightly acid; clear smooth boundary.

C4 30-44 ins. - Variable colors, extremely gravelly single grained

structures loose, nonsticky and nonplastic; no roots; 65 percent gravel;

slightly acid.

LOCATION AND SETTING: West-central Montana's tributary to Clark Fork River,

Sloway Gulch; S1/2 section 2, T. 17 N., R. 27 W., map unit 16.Ua. This soil is

formed in deltaic alluvium originating from ancient Lake Missoula. The parent

material is undifferentiated due to its mixed and varying origin. This soil

occurs on a 30 percent gradient with a south aspect at 3,440 feet elevation.

Selected characteristics for Typic Xerorthents are shown below. Surface

horizons on these soils are thin, organic, and have an average of 40 percent

less rock fragments than subsurface horizons. Subsurface horizons average 75

percent rock fragments. The sand content of these soils increases consistently

with depth. The parent material for these soils is undifferentiated as it

consists of ancient Lake Missoula beach deposits which are of variable origin.

-20-

Typic Xerorthents, sandy-skeletal are like the reference pedon. This is the

prevalent family for these soils. There is greater than 35 percent rock

fragment throughout the profile with the fine portion consisting mostly of sand

and at the most 10 percent clay.

The ranges of selected characteristics for Andeptic Udorthents are shown below.

These soils have a 7- to 12-inch deep ash mantle overlying a shallow eluvial

horizon. The parent material for these soils is undifferentiated as it consists

of the ash mantle over ancient Lake Missoula beach deposits which are of

variable origin. The sand content of these soils increases with depth, and clay

does not exceed 12 percent by volume. The percent of rock fragments doubles

from the surface horizon (ash mantle) to subsurface.

-23-

horizon (ash mantle) has less than 35 percent rock fragment. The subsurface

horizons contain more than 35 percent rock fragment.

classification. The profiles contain 35 or more percent rock fragments which
