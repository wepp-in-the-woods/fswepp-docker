MAP UNIT DESCRIPTION

M.U. 13.Ja

MAP UNIT SUMMARY

M.U. SETTING The landform consists of high stream terraces. Map units are

near the mouth of streams tributary to the Clark Fork River.

M.U. COMPONENTS Soils are formed in very cobbly alluvium with a thick surface

layer of silty lacustrine deposits. Surface soil textures are moderately fine

and subsoil textures are moderately coarse. Native vegetation is Douglas fir

and grand fir forests.

ADJOINING M.U. Adjacent units are recent floodplains and lower stream terraces

that support riparian vegetation or meadow and steep, rocky mountain slopes

with dense Douglas-fir or grand fir forests.

This map unit consists of high alluvial terraces well above the stream

floodplain. These old stream terraces have been dissected by valley downcutting

leaving these terraces perched above younger stream-formed landforms. These

terraces have gently sloping surfaces and steep escarpments on the downcut

side. Relief on the escarpments may vary from 30-200 feet.

Sediment delivery efficiency is low from the nearly level surfaces of this

landform but can be quite high from escarpments.

VEGETATION

HABITAT TYPE (HT) COMPOSITION The map unit consists of Douglas fir/dwarf

huckleberry (PSME/VACA), Douglas fir/twinflower (PSME/LIBO), and Douglas

fir/snowberry (PSME/SYAL) - 30 percent; grand fir/twinflower (ABGR/LIBO) and

grand fir/queencup beadlily (ABGR/CLUN) - 60 percent. Included HT with

dissimilar management implications is Douglas fir/Idaho fescue (PSME/FEID) - 10

percent.

HABITAT TYPE DISTRIBUTION AND CHARACTERISTICS PSME/VACA, PSME/LIBO, and

PSME/SYAL occur in a mosaic on southerly aspects throughout the unit. PSME/LIBO

occurrence is of small extent. A relatively warm and dry climate, with seasonal

cool air drainage, and a moderate timber productivity is associated with these

HTs. ABGR/LIBO and ABGR/CLUN occur on other than south aspects and indicate a

cool and moist climate and high timber productivity. An included HT of small

extent with similar management implications is subalpine fir/twinflower

(ABLA/LIBO).

Included are up to 10 percent dissimilar HTs. PSME/FEID occurs on the lowest

elevations of this map unit and has a lower productivity.

EXISTING VEGETATION is a Douglas fir and grand fir forest. Seral stands include

western larch, ponderosa pine and Douglas fir. The forest understory is

composed of dwarf huckleberry, kinnikinnick, pinegrass, snowberry, and Idaho

fescue on southerly aspects. Other aspects support grand fir, western larch,

Douglas fir, twinflower, queencup beadlily, red osier dogwood, serviceberry,

rocky mountain maple, and western meadowrue.

GEOLOGY

OCCURRENCE The unit is underlain by deep alluvium with a thick deposit of

lacustrine sediments on or near the surface. These unconsolidated materials were

deposited during times of heavy glacier melt and subsequently have been

partially eroded by valley downcutting. As a result, these materials occur high

above the existing stream channels and terrace-like features may or may not be

striking depending on subsequent downcutting.

Included with dissimilar properties are Belt supergroup metasedimentary bedrock

that outcrops where valley downcutting has exposed it. Soils are very shallow

and productivity is much lower.

SOILS

Typic Eutroboralfs, fine silty over loamy skeletal

GENERAL NATURE OF SOILS Soils are well drained and deep. Moderately fine

textures derived from lacustrine silts extend to a depth of 2 feet and have a

moderately slow permeability. Subsoils contain greater than 35 percent rock

fragments and are moderately coarse textured.

COMPOSITION The major soil has a silty clay loam surface layer, 2 feet thick,

formed in silty lacustrine deposits. The subsoil is a very cobbly sandy loam

formed in alluvial deposits. Included are similar soils with silty surface

layers containing less clay. These soils occupy 85 percent of the map unit.

Included are up to 15 percent dissimilar soils. Soils that have moderately

coarse textured surface layers instead of the moderately fine textured surface

occur near the edge of map unit boundaries and are less productive timber sites.

These soils are Typic Xerochrepts, loamy skeletal, mixed on south aspects and

Dystric Eutrochrepts, loamy skeletal, mixed on other aspects.

MANAGEMENT IMPLICATIONS

TIMBER This map unit is moderately suited to timber management. Potential annual

production is ____ cu.ft/A/yr or moderate on south aspects and high on other

aspects. Included terrace escarpments limit equipment operation due to slope

steepness and sediment delivery risk. Stream bottoms included in some map units

will require special harvest methods. Equipment operation can compact or rut the

soil under certain moisture conditions on the nearly flat surfaces. Operating

equipment only when the soil is dry or snow covered helps maintain soil

productivity. Seeding establishment is slowed by grass competition and moisture

stress on south aspects. Other aspects pose few regeneration problems.

ROADS The very cobbly sandy loam subsoil usually is exposed by construction and

cause roadbeds to be rough and irregular. Steep cutslopes in this material

tend to ravel. Unsurfaced roads built in the non-gravelly silty clay loam

surface material become rutted when wet and dusty when dry. Excavation below

silty clay loam surface, surfacing or seasonal closures help overcome these

limitations.

RANGE This map unit is well suited to range management. Potential forage

production is low to moderate. The soil is susceptible to compaction in the

spring. Grazing should be delayed until the soil is dry enough to support

livestock. Erosion will occur on trails made on terrace escarpments that access

stream bottoms.

WILDLIFE-FISHERIES

WATERSHED The major watershed management concern is controlling sediment

produced when terrace escarpments are undercut by streams or escarpments have

bee disturbed by management. The very cobbly sandy loam subsoils ravel when

exposed and are major sources of sediment. The terrace surfaces which are

nearly flat have low sediment delivery efficiency but consist of highly erodible

material which pose a potentially large sediment contribution if roads provide

transport points to the nearby streams.
